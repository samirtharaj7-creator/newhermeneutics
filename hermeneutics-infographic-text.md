# Hermeneutics Infographic Text

This document collects infographic-ready text for the General Hermeneutics steps and Special Hermeneutics genre panels.

## General Hermeneutics

### Overview Infographic: The Detective Method

Title: The Detective Method

Subtitle: A faithful way to read Scripture carefully.

Main copy:
Hermeneutics gives Bible study a faithful shape: prepare before you enter the passage, observe before deciding, interpret with context, enter the scene carefully, and apply with obedience. The goal is not to make Scripture say something useful, but to receive the meaning God gave through the human author and carry that meaning into life.

Method line:
Prepare. Observe. Interpret. Imagine. Apply.

Closing line:
We are not hunting for proof of what we already think. We are receiving what God has placed in the text.

### Step 1: Preparation

Action word: Pause

Short line:
Pray, slow down, and name assumptions before study.

Headline:
The investigator slows down before forming a theory.

Main copy:
Preparation is the pause at the doorway. Before you touch the passage, name the assumptions you carried in: what you already think, what you hope it says, and what you have been taught to hear. Pray for humility, hold those hunches loosely, and come ready to be corrected by the text rather than protected from it.

Bible study prompt:
Begin with prayerful humility. Name the assumptions, hopes, fears, and habits you bring to the passage, then hold them loosely before the Spirit and the text.

Key questions:
- What assumptions am I bringing to this passage?
- What do I hope this passage will say?
- What have I been taught to hear here?
- Am I willing to be corrected by the text?

Avoid:
- Solving the passage before reading it.
- Protecting a favorite idea from correction.
- Treating first impressions as final interpretation.

### Step 2: Observation

Action word: Notice

Short line:
Notice what the passage actually says before explaining it.

Headline:
The investigator works the room before explaining it.

Main copy:
Observation is the careful search of the scene. Before a theory is allowed to speak, the investigator records what is actually there: people, repeated words, questions, contrasts, commands, structure, tone, and anything unfamiliar. The more carefully you observe, the less you will guess when interpretation begins.

Bible study prompt:
Write down what the passage actually says before explaining it. Track people, repeated words, contrasts, commands, questions, structure, and anything unfamiliar.

Key questions:
- What words or ideas repeat?
- Who is speaking, acting, or receiving the action?
- What commands, contrasts, questions, or promises appear?
- What seems unfamiliar, surprising, or emphasized?

Avoid:
- Explaining the passage too soon.
- Skipping repeated details.
- Building interpretation on vague impressions.

### Step 3: Interpretation

Action word: Test

Short line:
Study context, genre, words, cross-references, and theology.

Headline:
The investigator tests explanations against the evidence.

Main copy:
Interpretation is where the evidence is tested. Ask which reading best accounts for the whole passage in its genre, historical setting, grammar, local context, cross-references, and the wider witness of Scripture. Any explanation that has to ignore a stubborn detail, flatten the genre, or dodge the context needs to be set aside.

Bible study prompt:
Interpretation asks which reading accounts for all the evidence. Genre, setting, context, grammar, key words, cross-references, and Scripture as a whole must fit together.

Key questions:
- What did the author intend to communicate?
- How does the local context shape the meaning?
- How does the genre guide the reading?
- What reading accounts for all the evidence?
- How does the whole witness of Scripture clarify this passage?

Avoid:
- Ignoring context.
- Flattening genre.
- Using cross-references to escape the passage.
- Choosing an interpretation that cannot carry all the details.

### Step 4: Imagination

Action word: Rebuild

Short line:
Enter the scene carefully without inventing meaning.

Headline:
The investigator reconstructs the scene without inventing facts.

Main copy:
Imagination reconstructs the scene without inventing the facts. Enter the world of the passage: its room, road, crowd, tension, emotion, and distance from your own world. Let the scene become vivid enough to feel, but keep every detail tethered to the evidence the text actually gives.

Bible study prompt:
Enter the passage's world carefully, as the first hearers would have received it. Feel its force, then notice the distance between their situation and ours.

Key questions:
- What would this have felt like to the first hearers or readers?
- What setting, pressure, conflict, or emotion does the text reveal?
- What details are actually in the text?
- Where must I stop because the text does not say more?

Avoid:
- Inventing details.
- Treating imagination as interpretation.
- Making the passage vivid at the cost of accuracy.

### Step 5: Application

Action word: Act

Short line:
Bring the meaning forward into faithful obedience.

Headline:
The investigator writes the report and acts on the findings.

Main copy:
Application is the report that turns investigation into obedience. State the meaning clearly, draw forward the faithful principle, and name one concrete step you can actually take this week. The study is not finished when the passage is understood; it is finished when the truth begins to move into life.

Bible study prompt:
Finish with two findings: the verdict and the action. Write one plain sentence stating what the passage means in context, then choose one concrete, checkable step of obedience because the passage is true.

Key questions:
- What does this passage mean in its own context?
- What faithful principle carries forward?
- What response does this truth call for today?
- What concrete step can I take this week?

Avoid:
- Applying before interpreting.
- Staying vague.
- Turning study into information without obedience.

## Special Hermeneutics

### Special Hermeneutics Overview

Title: Read by Genre

Subtitle: Different biblical genres ask different interpretive questions.

Main copy:
Special Hermeneutics helps readers honor the kind of literature they are reading. Poetry, narrative, Gospel, epistle, law, parable, prophecy, and wisdom all communicate truly, but they do not all communicate in the same way. Identify the genre, look for the right clues, avoid the common mistakes, and then move toward faithful interpretation and application.

Method line:
Identify the genre. Observe its clues. Use its rules. Interpret faithfully. Apply carefully.

### Poetry

Title: Poetry

Summary:
Poetry guides worship, lament, wisdom, longing, and hope through imagery, structure, repetition, and emotional movement.

Rules:
- Master the Structure of Parallelism.
- Decode the Imagery and Figures of Speech.
- Identify the Speaker, Audience, and Voice Shifts.
- Interpret Images in Clusters, Not Isolation.
- Read Poetry as Worship and Prayer Before Proposition.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Where does this poem sit in worship, lament, wisdom, exile, kingship, or personal prayer?
- Observation: List repeated words, refrains, images, voice shifts, parallel lines, and emotional turns.
- Genre Rules: Which poetic rules most shape your reading, and why?
- Interpretation: State the poem's main movement in your own words without flattening its emotion.
- Application: How does this poem train prayer, worship, grief, hope, repentance, or trust today?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid treating imagery woodenly or skipping lament.

### Historical Narrative

Title: Historical Narrative

Summary:
Historical narrative is theological history: selected and arranged events that reveal God, covenant, character, conflict, and redemptive movement.

Rules:
- Narrative Has Three Levels.
- Trace the Plot Movement.
- Distinguish Between Descriptive and Prescriptive.
- Identify the Narrator's Theological Emphasis.
- Read Old Testament Narrative Through Christ Responsibly.
- Read Characters Through Contrast, Dialogue, and Development.
- Treat Speeches, Prayers, and Songs as Interpretive Clues.
- Use Historical, Cultural, Political, and Geographical Background Carefully.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Where does this story sit in the book, covenant setting, and larger biblical storyline?
- Observation: Track setting, main characters, central conflict, resolution, small details, repeated themes, dialogue, and what the story reveals about God.
- Genre Rules: Which narrative rules help you avoid turning the story into a shallow moral example?
- Interpretation: What theological emphasis does the narrator want readers to see?
- Application: What enduring principle carries forward without copying every ancient action?
- Common Mistakes: Do not treat every descriptive detail as a direct command.

### Gospels and Acts

Title: Gospels and Acts

Summary:
The Gospels reveal Jesus' identity and mission; Acts shows the risen Jesus continuing His mission through the Spirit-empowered church.

Rules:
- Read the Gospels as Theological Biography.
- Read Acts as Luke's Second Volume.
- Follow Each Gospel's Distinct Portrait of Jesus.
- Watch Old Testament Fulfillment.
- Trace the Movement from Jesus to Mission.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Where does this episode sit in the Gospel or Acts movement?
- Observation: Note setting, main characters, conflict, resolution, dialogue, fulfillment clues, responses to Jesus, Spirit activity, and mission movement.
- Genre Rules: Which Gospels and Acts rules help you read the episode on its own terms?
- Interpretation: How does this passage reveal Jesus, fulfillment, discipleship, witness, or the Spirit's mission?
- Application: What response of faith, repentance, witness, worship, or obedience does the passage call for?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid flattening the Gospel writer's emphasis or turning Acts into a mechanical rulebook.

### Epistles

Title: Epistles

Summary:
Epistles are occasional letters that teach through argument, pastoral correction, gospel logic, and Spirit-shaped application.

Rules:
- Read the Letter as Occasional Communication.
- Follow the Whole-Letter Movement.
- Trace the Paragraph Logic.
- Let Gospel Indicatives Ground Imperatives.
- Cross the Principlizing Bridge Carefully.
- Apply to the Church, Not Only Isolated Individuals.
- Recognize Rhetorical and Literary Forms.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Who wrote the letter, who received it, when was it written, and what situation, problem, question, or pastoral concern might it address?
- Observation: Track plain sense, unknown terms, difficult statements, Old Testament citations, conjunctions, commands, reasons, contrasts, repeated words, grammar, and paragraph flow.
- Genre Rules: Which epistle rules help you move from verse to argument?
- Interpretation: What is the author's main argument in this paragraph, and is he encouraging, correcting, warning, instructing, reminding, or defending?
- Application: How does the gospel ground the command or response today, and what principle carries from the original church to us?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid proof-texting, moralism, and speculative background reconstruction.

### Law

Title: Law

Summary:
Biblical law reveals God's values, covenant holiness, justice, worship, neighbor love, and moral wisdom fulfilled through Christ.

Rules:
- Recognize the Traditional Distinctions.
- Principlize for Contemporary Application.
- Start with Values Before Laws.
- Distinguish Regulation from Endorsement.
- Carry the Law Forward Through Christlike Love.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Where does this law sit in covenant life, worship, justice, holiness, or community protection?
- Observation: Identify the command form, stated reason, protected value, people affected, and covenant setting.
- Genre Rules: Which law rules help you move from ancient form to enduring value?
- Interpretation: What value or theological principle is the law teaching?
- Application: How does this value carry forward through Christlike love today?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid legalistic copying, careless dismissal, or confusing regulation with endorsement.

### Parables

Title: Parables

Summary:
Parables are vivid kingdom stories that expose assumptions, surprise hearers, and demand a response.

Rules:
- Seek the Central Truth and Avoid Over-Allegorizing.
- Identify the Original Audience and Occasion.
- Look for the Hook or Unexpected Twist.
- Read the Parable in its Gospel Context.
- Let the Parable Demand a Response.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: Who hears the parable, what prompts it, and where does it sit in the Gospel?
- Observation: Track main characters, conflict, surprise, reversal, cultural details, and the final punch.
- Genre Rules: Which parable rules protect the central point from over-allegorizing?
- Interpretation: What central truth is Jesus pressing on the hearers?
- Application: What response does this parable demand from disciples today?
- Common Mistakes: Do not turn incidental details into major doctrine.

### Prophecy

Title: Prophecy

Summary:
Prophecy speaks covenant lawsuit, judgment, hope, restoration, and apocalyptic vision with Christ, worship, endurance, and God's kingdom at the center.

Rules:
- First Identify the Kind of Prophecy.
- Let Classical Prophecy Function as Covenant Lawsuit.
- Watch for Conditional Prophecy.
- Recognize Near-View and Far-View Fulfillment.
- Let Scripture Decode Prophetic Symbols.
- Trace Old Testament Quotations, Citations, Allusions, and Echoes.
- Use the Historicist Framework for Daniel and Revelation.
- Follow the Continuous Historical Sequence.
- Use Recapitulation: Repeat and Enlarge.
- Apply the Day-Year Principle Carefully.
- Recognize Literary and Rhetorical Design.
- Keep Christ, the Sanctuary, and the Great Controversy at the Center.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: What kind of prophecy is this: covenant lawsuit, judgment oracle, restoration promise, or apocalyptic vision?
- Observation: Track symbols, sequence, covenant charges, Old Testament quotations or echoes, time markers, literary patterns, heavenly scenes, repeated visions, and explanations inside the text.
- Genre Rules: Which prophetic rules keep your interpretation text-governed and Christ-centered?
- Interpretation: What does this prophecy reveal about God's rule, judgment, mercy, Christ, worship, or final restoration?
- Application: How should this passage form faithfulness, repentance, endurance, mission, or hope today?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid speculation, headline-reading, arbitrary symbol use, ignored literary structure, and timeline confusion.

### Wisdom Literature

Title: Wisdom Literature

Summary:
Wisdom literature forms people who fear the Lord, discern life well, speak rightly, endure suffering, and live with humility.

Rules:
- Identify the Wisdom Form Before Applying It.
- Read Proverbs as General Wisdom, Not Mechanical Promises.
- Keep the Fear of the Lord at the Center.
- Let the Wisdom Books Balance One Another.
- Apply Wisdom as Character Formation.

Infographic prompts:
- Context: What wisdom form is this, and who is speaking?
- Observation: Notice contrasts, comparisons, repeated themes, poetic structure, speaker, and life situation.
- Genre Rules: Which wisdom rules keep this from becoming a mechanical promise or self-help slogan?
- Interpretation: What kind of wise character or reverent discernment is the text forming?
- Application: How should this wisdom shape speech, habits, humility, work, relationships, or worship?
- Common Mistakes: Avoid overpromising, misquoting, or speaker-confusion.
