# General Hermeneutics Infographic-Ready Text

A concise source packet for one overview infographic and five focused phase infographics.

## General Hermeneutics

### Overview Infographic: The Detective Method

Title: The Detective Method

Subtitle: A faithful way to read Scripture carefully.

Main copy:
Hermeneutics gives Bible study a faithful shape: prepare before you enter the passage, observe before deciding, interpret with context, enter the scene carefully, and apply with obedience. The goal is not to make Scripture say something useful, but to receive the meaning God gave through the human author and carry that meaning into life.

Method line:
Prepare. Observe. Interpret. Imagine. Apply.

Closing line:
We are not hunting for proof of what we already think. We are receiving what God has placed in the text.

### Step 1: Preparation

Action word: Pause

Short line:
Pray, slow down, and name assumptions before study.

Headline:
The investigator slows down before forming a theory.

Main copy:
Preparation is the pause at the doorway. Before you touch the passage, name the assumptions you carried in: what you already think, what you hope it says, and what you have been taught to hear. Pray for humility, hold those hunches loosely, and come ready to be corrected by the text rather than protected from it.

Bible study prompt:
Begin with prayerful humility. Name the assumptions, hopes, fears, and habits you bring to the passage, then hold them loosely before the Spirit and the text.

Key questions:
- What assumptions am I bringing to this passage?
- What do I hope this passage will say?
- What have I been taught to hear here?
- Am I willing to be corrected by the text?

Avoid:
- Solving the passage before reading it.
- Protecting a favorite idea from correction.
- Treating first impressions as final interpretation.

### Step 2: Observation

Action word: Notice

Short line:
Notice what the passage actually says before explaining it.

Headline:
The investigator works the room before explaining it.

Main copy:
Observation is the careful search of the scene. Before a theory is allowed to speak, the investigator records what is actually there: people, repeated words, questions, contrasts, commands, structure, tone, and anything unfamiliar. The more carefully you observe, the less you will guess when interpretation begins.

Bible study prompt:
Write down what the passage actually says before explaining it. Track people, repeated words, contrasts, commands, questions, structure, and anything unfamiliar.

Key questions:
- What words or ideas repeat?
- Who is speaking, acting, or receiving the action?
- What commands, contrasts, questions, or promises appear?
- What seems unfamiliar, surprising, or emphasized?

Avoid:
- Explaining the passage too soon.
- Skipping repeated details.
- Building interpretation on vague impressions.

### Step 3: Interpretation

Action word: Test

Short line:
Study context, genre, words, cross-references, and theology.

Headline:
The investigator tests explanations against the evidence.

Main copy:
Interpretation is where the evidence is tested. Ask which reading best accounts for the whole passage in its genre, historical setting, grammar, local context, cross-references, and the wider witness of Scripture. Any explanation that has to ignore a stubborn detail, flatten the genre, or dodge the context needs to be set aside.

Bible study prompt:
Interpretation asks which reading accounts for all the evidence. Genre, setting, context, grammar, key words, cross-references, and Scripture as a whole must fit together.

Key questions:
- What did the author intend to communicate?
- How does the local context shape the meaning?
- How does the genre guide the reading?
- What reading accounts for all the evidence?
- How does the whole witness of Scripture clarify this passage?

Avoid:
- Ignoring context.
- Flattening genre.
- Using cross-references to escape the passage.
- Choosing an interpretation that cannot carry all the details.

### Step 4: Imagination

Action word: Rebuild

Short line:
Enter the scene carefully without inventing meaning.

Headline:
The investigator reconstructs the scene without inventing facts.

Main copy:
Imagination reconstructs the scene without inventing the facts. Enter the world of the passage: its room, road, crowd, tension, emotion, and distance from your own world. Let the scene become vivid enough to feel, but keep every detail tethered to the evidence the text actually gives.

Bible study prompt:
Enter the passage's world carefully, as the first hearers would have received it. Feel its force, then notice the distance between their situation and ours.

Key questions:
- What would this have felt like to the first hearers or readers?
- What setting, pressure, conflict, or emotion does the text reveal?
- What details are actually in the text?
- Where must I stop because the text does not say more?

Avoid:
- Inventing details.
- Treating imagination as interpretation.
- Making the passage vivid at the cost of accuracy.

### Step 5: Application

Action word: Act

Short line:
Bring the meaning forward into faithful obedience.

Headline:
The investigator writes the report and acts on the findings.

Main copy:
Application is the report that turns investigation into obedience. State the meaning clearly, draw forward the faithful principle, and name one concrete step you can actually take this week. The study is not finished when the passage is understood; it is finished when the truth begins to move into life.

Bible study prompt:
Finish with two findings: the verdict and the action. Write one plain sentence stating what the passage means in context, then choose one concrete, checkable step of obedience because the passage is true.

Key questions:
- What does this passage mean in its own context?
- What faithful principle carries forward?
- What response does this truth call for today?
- What concrete step can I take this week?

Avoid:
- Applying before interpreting.
- Staying vague.
- Turning study into information without obedience.
